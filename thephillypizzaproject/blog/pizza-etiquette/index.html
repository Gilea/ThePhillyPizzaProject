Pizza Etiquette: How to Eat Pizza Like a Pro

🗓 July 2025⏱ 6 min
🍕

There are no universal rules for eating pizza. But there are strong local conventions, and violating them — even unknowingly — can mark you as an outsider in a city that cares deeply about its food culture. Here's what you need to know about eating pizza in Philadelphia.

Fold, Don't Fork

If someone next to you is eating a New York style slice with a fork and knife, you are watching a person do something deeply wrong. The fold is mandatory. The correct technique: pick up the slice by the crust (not the point), fold it lengthwise so that the two edges of the crust meet, and eat from the point inward. The fold accomplishes several things: it reinforces the structural integrity of the slice, it concentrates the toppings toward the point for maximum first-bite impact, and it allows the grease to run toward the point rather than dripping off the sides.

Neapolitan pizza is a different matter. A properly made Neapolitan pizza — with its soft, wet center — is often eaten with utensils, or folded into a "libretto" (book fold) or "portafoglio" (wallet fold). Both are acceptable. The portafoglio, which folds the pizza into quarters, is the street-food version that allows you to eat it while walking. At a sit-down restaurant, use whichever approach works for the particular pizza in front of you.

Taking Photos

We have a rule on the team: one good shot, then eat it. Pizza is a hot food that degrades quickly once it comes out of the oven. A Neapolitan pizza that sits for three minutes while you compose the perfect Instagram shot is a slightly worse pizza than the one that gets eaten immediately. Take your photo. Make it quick. Then eat the pizza at its best.

The exception: if the presentation is genuinely exceptional (Sorellina's Stella pizza, for example, was visually stunning), take the time to do it justice. But even then — one minute, maximum.

Sharing and Ordering

The correct way to eat pizza at a sit-down Neapolitan restaurant is to order multiple pizzas and share them. One pizza per person is the tourist approach — you end up eating the same pizza for the entire meal, which means you get one data point about the kitchen when you could have four. Order two or three pizzas for a table of two. Order four for a table of three or four (which is exactly how we operate). Taste everything. Have opinions. Argue.

BYOB Culture

Philadelphia's BYOB restaurant culture extends to pizza. Many of the best pizza spots in the city are unlicensed, which means you bring your own wine, beer, or whatever you want to drink. This is not a hardship — it's actually one of the best things about eating in Philly. You get to choose exactly what you want to drink, you pay wholesale prices instead of restaurant markups, and the total bill is significantly lower than it would be at a licensed restaurant.

The etiquette: stop at a wine or beer shop before you arrive. Don't show up empty-handed at a BYOB and ask if there's somewhere nearby to buy wine — that's a rookie move. Bring glasses if you're going somewhere very casual (though most BYOBs have glasses). And tip generously: the server isn't making anything on beverage sales.

Tipping at Pizza Restaurants

Standard Philadelphia restaurant tipping applies: 20% is the baseline at a sit-down restaurant with table service, regardless of whether it's a white-tablecloth place or a pizza joint. Counter service spots don't require a tip, though it's appreciated. Delivery: at least $3-5, more if the weather is bad or the order is large.

The Cornicione Question

The cornicione — the outer crust ring on a Neapolitan pizza — is not a handle. It is food. If the pizza is made correctly, the cornicione should be light, airy, slightly charred on the outside, and soft on the inside. It should taste like good bread. Eat it. If you're leaving the cornicione on the plate at a Neapolitan restaurant, you're either eating a pizza with a bad cornicione (in which case, note it in your mental critique) or you're committing a pizza sin.
