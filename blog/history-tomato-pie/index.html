The History of Tomato Pie: Philadelphia's Forgotten Pizza

🗓 July 2025⏱ 9 min ✍️ The Philly Pizza Project
🍕

If you ask most Americans about America's contribution to pizza history, they'll say New York. The thin, foldable coal-fired slice. The pizzerias of Little Italy. The 99-cent slices of Midtown. And that story is real and worth telling. But there's another American pizza origin story that gets far less attention, and it happens to be ours: the tomato pie of the Delaware Valley.

What Is Tomato Pie?

Tomato pie is, to a first approximation, a pizza made backwards. The sauce goes on top. Not on top of the toppings — on top of the cheese, applied as the final layer before and sometimes after baking. It's typically served at room temperature, not hot. The crust is thicker and breadier than a New York slice, more reminiscent of focaccia than a pizza shell. And it's frequently sold in bakeries rather than pizzerias — by the square, wrapped in paper, taken home.

The "tomato pie" tradition is concentrated in two places: Trenton, New Jersey, and Philadelphia, Pennsylvania. Both cities have strong claims to the form, and the origin debate between them is never fully resolved. What's not debatable is that the Delaware Valley — the cultural and geographic region defined by the Delaware River and its communities on both banks — is where this food lives.

The Italian Roots

To understand tomato pie, you need to understand where the Italian immigrants who created it came from. The great wave of Italian immigration to the Delaware Valley came primarily from Southern Italy: Campania, Sicily, Calabria, and Basilicata. These immigrants brought with them the pizza traditions of their home regions — but those traditions were not the Neapolitan pizza we know today.

In the late 19th century, Neapolitan pizza was still a street food for the urban poor. It was simple, cheap, and variable. The immigrants who came to Philadelphia and Trenton didn't bring with them the DOC-certified, wood-fired, leopard-spotted pizza that has since become the global standard for "authentic" Neapolitan pizza. They brought older, more vernacular traditions: bread baked with tomatoes, olive oil-enriched doughs, the practice of using the bakery oven to bake whatever needed baking.

The tomato pie is what happened when those vernacular traditions met American ingredients, American ovens, and the specific conditions of immigrant life in an East Coast industrial city.

Philadelphia's Italian Market

The center of Italian-American life in Philadelphia was — and in many ways still is — the Italian Market on South 9th Street in South Philadelphia. Established in the late 19th century, the Italian Market was the commercial heart of a neighborhood where Southern Italian immigrants had settled in large numbers. By the early 20th century, it was one of the most concentrated Italian-American communities in the country.

The bakeries of this neighborhood became the first producers of what we now call tomato pie. They were baking bread every day, and the pizza — or "tomato pie" — was a bakery product, not a restaurant product. It was made in the same ovens as the bread, with the same dough (often enriched with olive oil), and sold from the same counters.

This is a crucial distinction: Philadelphia's tomato pie emerged from the bakery tradition, not the restaurant tradition. It was everyday food. Cheap food. Food that families bought at room temperature and took home, or ate standing at a counter. Not date-night food, not special-occasion food — daily bread, in the most literal sense.

Trenton vs. Philadelphia

Forty miles up the Delaware River, Trenton, New Jersey, was developing its own version of the tomato pie at roughly the same time. The Trenton tomato pie has a few distinct characteristics: the cheese goes on the dough first, then the toppings, then the sauce on top. This "upside-down" construction is the defining feature of the Trenton style and is probably the original tomato pie configuration.

Philadelphia's version evolved somewhat differently. Some spots apply sauce before cheese, some after. Some apply sauce both before and after (a technique that maximizes the tomato impact without losing the structural function of the cheese). The Philadelphia tomato pie is generally a bit thicker and breadier than the Trenton version, and it's more likely to be found at room temperature rather than served hot.

The debate over which city originated the tomato pie is entertaining but ultimately unresolvable. Both traditions are real, both are old, and both deserve to exist.

The 20th Century: Tomato Pie Goes Underground

For much of the 20th century, the tomato pie stayed local. While New York-style pizza was spreading nationally through chains and franchises, the Delaware Valley tomato pie remained a hyperlocal tradition, understood by locals and invisible to outsiders. You didn't find it in guidebooks. National food media didn't cover it. It just existed — in neighborhood bakeries, in church fundraisers, at family events.

This invisibility may have been protective. The tomato pie didn't get homogenized because it didn't get noticed. The people who made it kept making it the same way their parents and grandparents had made it, without pressure to modernize or adapt for a national audience.

Revival and Recognition

In the 2010s, something shifted. Food media started paying attention to regional American food traditions that had been overlooked. The New York Times wrote about Trenton tomato pie. Food52 ran pieces on Philadelphia's bakery pizza tradition. Serious Eats dispatched writers to both cities.

And within Philadelphia, a new generation of bakers and pizza makers started engaging seriously with the tomato pie tradition — both out of genuine cultural pride and as a point of differentiation in an increasingly crowded market.

Tomato Pie and the Broader Philadelphia Pizza Story

We think about the tomato pie tradition constantly as we work on The Philly Pizza Project. It's a reminder that pizza is not one thing — it's a family of related foods with different histories, different technical requirements, and different cultural meanings.

The best Philadelphia pizza exists in dialogue with this history, whether the people making it are consciously aware of it or not. When a South Philly bakery sells a room-temperature square of thick-crust pizza with sauce on top for two dollars, they're doing something that connects directly to the immigrant bakers of the early 20th century. And when a new-wave Neapolitan spot in Center City obsesses over fermentation times and tomato provenance, they're participating in a different strand of the same Italian-American food story.

Both traditions matter. Both are worth understanding. And Philadelphia is one of the few cities in America where you can taste both in the same afternoon.
